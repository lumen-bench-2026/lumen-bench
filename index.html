Headline findings

Four systematic failure modes

From the 207,360-observation panel: each finding is backed by a paired-Wilcoxon or logistic-regression test reported in the paper.

1

Cross-lingual safety gap

Hindi users face higher violation rates than English users on identical scenarios. The most extreme single-model gap reaches 39 percentage points.

3.6× HI/EN under ethical prompt
2

Concentrated in budget tier

The cross-lingual gap collapses among premium models and concentrates in the budget tier — exactly where mobile-app and edge deployments live.

Q4 premium: 0.34% violation
3

Ethical priming dominates

Replacing the ethical system prompt with a neutral one raises violation odds by an order of magnitude — the largest single effect in the panel.

OR ≈ 12× neutral vs. ethical
4

The say-do gap

In 8.18% of substantive tool calls, the model verbalizes a refusal while the call still executes the harm. In Llama-4-Scout this reaches 51% of violations.

8.18% panel-wide

Methodology in 60 seconds

How a case is scored

①

Scenario

One of 108 native-authored requests asking the assistant to do something ethically wrong but operationally plausible.

②

Tool call

Model receives 21 deliberately generic tools. Refusal must come from reasoning, not lexical pattern matching on tool names.

③

Judge

An LLM judge (o4-mini) classifies into COMPLY, SAY_DO_GAP, REDIRECT, or AMBIGUOUS. Validated against a 100-case human anchor (κ=0.73) and three cross-judges (Fleiss' κ=0.78).

④

Sandbox

Stored tool calls are replayed against a deterministic mock organization. 74.6% of L2-positive cases produce non-trivial state change.
